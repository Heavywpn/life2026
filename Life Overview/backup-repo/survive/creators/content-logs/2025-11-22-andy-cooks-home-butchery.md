## Content Summary

Comprehensive tutorial on breaking down five beef subprimals into steaks at home. Demonstrates professional butchery techniques adapted for home cooks to achieve ~50% cost savings vs buying pre-cut steaks. Emphasizes control over steak thickness for proper "cuisson" (French term for desired doneness) and zero-waste approach with four-category trim system.

### Core Philosophy
> "That's the beauty of cutting steaks yourself - you can control the thickness. Those scrangly supermarket steaks about this thick - really hard to get good cuisson. Can't get both a good crust and not overcook the inside."

2. Fat trimmings (for tallow)

## Skills Demonstrated in Video

### Universal Butchery Principles (17 skills total)

1. **Seam butchery** - Follow natural muscle separation
   - "You could literally pull this apart"
   - Work with anatomy, not against it

2. **Silver skin removal** - Angled knife technique
   - Point knife under, angle up
   - Minimal meat loss when pulled off

3. **Fat cap management** - Even trimming for uniform rendering
   - Can trim whole or per-steak (Andy prefers per-steak on expensive cuts)

4. **Grain direction recognition** - Cut across grain for tenderness
   - Critical for all cuts
   - Short grain already tender, long grain must be cut across

5. **Steak portioning control** - 2+ fingers thick (300-400g)
   - Solves supermarket "too thin" problem
   - Enables both crust and proper doneness

6. **The chain removal** - Optional trim piece on sirloin/fillet
   - Restaurant: remove (looks messy)
   - Home: can leave or use for trim

7. **Tallow rendering** - Best cooking fat for steaks
   - Cup of water, medium heat
   - Lower heat when water evaporates
   - Render until clear/golden

8. **Picanha separation** - Rump cap removal
   - Seam butchery technique
   - Multiple cooking options

9. **Ribeye cap preservation** - Don't remove!
   - "Tastiest part of the animal"
   - Keep attached to eye

10. **Rib remnant removal** - From ribeye cap
    - Where bones used to attach
    - "Like silver skin at another level"

11. **Porterhouse vs T-bone** - Fillet measurement
    - Porterhouse: 2.5cm+ fillet at bone
    - T-bone: ~1cm fillet

12. **Knife-first, saw-second** - Bone cutting technique
    - Cut meat with knife as much as possible
    - Only saw through bone
    - "Don't cut muscle with saw - makes horrible job"

13. **Stir fry meat processing** - From trim
    - Fillet chain (very lean, fast cooking)
    - Rump trim (across grain)
    - "Stir fry a fillet steak in this economy? What are you crazy?"

14. **Roast beef prep** - From rump section without fat cap
    - Roast hot and rare
    - Refrigerate overnight
    - Lasts 3-4 days
    - Slice thin for sandwiches

15. **Surface preparation** - Safety technique
    - Pat dry before cutting
    - "If sliding around, it's dangerous"

16. **Face squaring** - Start with clean edge
    - Two schools: start wonky or square first
    - Andy prefers squared first cut

17. **End piece utilization** - Nothing wasted
    - Medium-well orders (more connective tissue)
    - Minute steaks
    - Personal lunch

## Universal Techniques Applicable to Other Proteins

### Seam Butchery
- Works on all animals (beef, lamb, pork, game)
- Follow natural muscle boundaries
- Can feel seams before cutting

### Silver Skin Management
- Same technique across all proteins
- Tough membrane on all muscles
- Angle knife up, minimal meat loss

### Grain Direction
- Critical for tenderness
- Identify before cutting
- Short vs long grain recognition

### Fat Rendering
- Applicable to all animal fats
- Pork (lard), chicken (schmaltz), beef (tallow)
- Same basic process (water + low heat)

## Marbling Reality Check

### Social Media vs Reality
> "This is not a really highly marbled steak and that is fine. There is nothing wrong with steaks that have this amount of marbling. We've kind of been spoiled lately on social media. Everyone shows these crazy marbled steaks all the time. And I'm completely guilty of it myself, but this is the steak I grew up eating and there's nothing wrong with it."

**Message**: Don't chase Instagram-level marbling. Normal marbling is perfectly good.

## Supermarket Steak Critique

### The Problem
> "Those scrangly supermarket steaks that are about this thick or even thinner - it's really hard to get a good cuisson. You can't get both a good crust and not overcook the inside."

**Even with good beef**: Thickness matters more than premium labeling

### The Solution
- Buy subprimals
- Portion yourself at 2+ fingers thick
- Control your own cuisson
- Save 50% cost
