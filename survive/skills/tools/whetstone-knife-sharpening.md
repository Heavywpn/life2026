## Overview

Manual knife sharpening using Japanese whetstones is a fundamental skill that produces the sharpest possible edge on any blade. Beyond the practical result, the process itself embodies intentional living - slowing down, focusing on technique, and finding satisfaction in manual craftsmanship.

**Philosophy**: The process of doing simple things manually makes a better life. Sharpening is meditation with a purpose.

## Grit Progression Guide

| Grit | Purpose | When to Use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **400** | Repair/reshape | Damaged, chipped, or very dull blades |
| **1000** | Establish edge | Regular sharpening, dull knives |
| **3000** | Refine | Remove scratches, improve edge |
| **8000** | Polish | Mirror finish, razor edge |
| **Strop** | Final polish | Remove burr, align edge |

**Rule**: Start at the coarsest grit needed, then progress through each finer grit.
- Slightly dull: Start at 1000
- Very dull: Start at 400
- Maintenance: Start at 3000

## Step-by-Step Process

### Preparation (5 minutes)

1. **Soak stones** - Submerge in water for 5-10 minutes
   - Bubbles will rise; wait until they stop
   - Splash stones stay wet during use (don't need pre-soaking)

2. **Set up workspace**
   - Stable, flat surface
   - Towel underneath to catch water
   - Good lighting to see the edge

3. **Identify blade angle**
   - Japanese knives: 15° per side
   - Western/German knives: 20° per side
   - Pocket knives: Usually 20°
   - When in doubt: Match existing angle

### Sharpening Process

**Step 1: Coarse Stone (400 grit)** - Only if needed
```
Purpose: Remove material, repair chips, establish new edge
Time: 2-3 minutes per side
```
1. Place knife at correct angle (use angle guide initially)
2. Apply light, consistent pressure
3. Push edge forward across stone (edge leading)
4. Maintain angle throughout stroke
5. 10-15 strokes per side, alternating
6. Feel for burr on opposite side (indicates edge formed)

**Step 2: Medium Stone (1000 grit)**
```
Purpose: Sharpen, create working edge
Time: 2-3 minutes per side
```
1. Same technique as coarse
2. Lighter pressure than 400 grit
3. 15-20 strokes per side
4. Burr should form on opposite side
5. Alternate sides to even out burr

**Step 3: Fine Stone (3000 grit)**
```
Purpose: Refine edge, remove scratches
Time: 1-2 minutes per side
```
1. Very light pressure
2. 10-15 strokes per side
3. Edge should start to feel sharp

**Step 4: Polish Stone (8000 grit)**
```
Purpose: Mirror polish, razor edge
Time: 1-2 minutes per side
```
1. Minimal pressure (weight of knife only)
2. 10-15 strokes per side
3. Edge should reflect light uniformly

**Step 5: Strop**
```
Purpose: Remove burr, align edge, final polish
Time: 30 seconds - 1 minute
```
1. Spine leading (opposite of sharpening direction)
2. Very light pressure
3. 10-20 strokes per side
4. Edge should be razor sharp

### Testing the Edge

**Paper Test**
- Hold paper vertically
- Slice downward at slight angle
- Sharp knife: Clean cut with no tearing
- Dull knife: Catches, tears, or won't cut

**Tomato Test**
- Slice without pressure
- Sharp knife: Glides through skin
- Dull knife: Squashes before cutting

**Hair Test** (advanced)
- Shave arm hair
- Razor sharp: Cuts hair cleanly

## Angle Guide

### Finding the Right Angle

**The Sharpie Trick:**
1. Color the edge bevel with permanent marker
2. Make a few strokes on stone
3. Check where marker was removed
4. Adjust angle until full bevel contacts stone

**Angle Reference:**
```
15° = Two stacked coins under spine (thin knives)
20° = Three stacked coins under spine (thick knives)

Finger technique: Place fingertips on blade, feel for
consistent contact across the bevel
```

### Common Knife Angles

| Knife Type | Angle (per side) | Total Edge Angle |
|------------|------------------|------------------|
| Japanese chef (Santoku, Gyuto) | 15° | 30° |
| German chef (Wüsthof, Henckels) | 20° | 40° |
| Pocket/EDC knife | 20° | 40° |
| Outdoor/survival knife | 20-25° | 40-50° |
| Cleaver | 25° | 50° |

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Inconsistent Angle
**Problem**: Edge is uneven, doesn't get sharp
**Solution**: Use angle guide, practice muscle memory, use Sharpie trick

### Mistake 2: Too Much Pressure
**Problem**: Gouges in stone, uneven edge, slow progress
**Solution**: Let the abrasive do the work; light, consistent pressure

### Mistake 3: Skipping Grits
**Problem**: Scratches remain, edge not refined
**Solution**: Progress through each grit; don't skip steps

### Mistake 4: Not Forming a Burr
**Problem**: Not actually sharpening, just polishing dull edge
**Solution**: Work coarse stone until you feel burr on opposite side

### Mistake 5: Dry Stone
**Problem**: Steel clogs stone, inconsistent sharpening
**Solution**: Keep stone wet; splash water frequently during use

### Mistake 6: Lifting on Backstroke
**Problem**: Rounds over the edge
**Solution**: Maintain contact throughout stroke, or lift intentionally

## Tips & Tricks

### Technique Tips
- **Count strokes**: Equal strokes per side ensures even edge
- **Full blade contact**: Use entire length of stone
- **Consistent speed**: Slow, controlled strokes beat fast ones
- **Feel the burr**: Run finger FROM spine TO edge (never along edge)

### Stone Care
- **Flatten regularly**: Stones dish (curve) with use; flatten monthly
- **Store dry**: Let stones dry completely before storage
- **Never drop**: Whetstones chip easily
- **Dedicate stones**: Don't use kitchen knife stone for garden tools

### Efficiency Tips
- **Batch sharpen**: Do all knives in one session while stones are soaked
- **Maintenance sharpening**: Quick touch-up (3000 → 8000 → strop) monthly
- **Full sharpening**: Complete progression quarterly or as needed

## Stone Maintenance

### Flattening Stones

Over time, whetstones develop a dish (concave surface) from uneven wear.

**When to flatten**:
- Visible curve in stone
- Water pools in center
- Every 5-10 full sharpening sessions

**How to flatten**:
1. Use flattening stone (diamond plate or dedicated flattener)
2. Draw pencil grid on stone surface
3. Rub in figure-8 pattern until grid is gone
4. Rinse and continue
