# Client Prospect Presentations

## Audience Profile

### Who They Are
- Business owners or decision-makers
- Frustrated with current IT situation
- May or may not be tech-savvy
- Looking for solutions, not more problems
- Evaluating you against other providers

### What They Care About
- Will this solve my problem?
- Can I trust this person?
- What will this cost?
- Will they be there when I need them?
- Am I making the right decision?

### Their Fears
- Getting locked into wrong solution
- Hidden costs
- Being talked down to
- Downtime and disruption
- Making expensive mistake

---

## Presentation Goals

### Primary
1. Build trust and credibility
2. Understand their specific situation
3. Demonstrate you're different
4. Show clear path forward
5. Earn next step/commitment

### Secondary
- Educate without overwhelming
- Surface hidden concerns
- Differentiate from competitors
- Set expectations clearly

---

## Your Unique Positioning

### The Difference
"Most IT providers see you as a contract. I see you as someone who deserves a fair fight in technology."

### Trust Builders
- Military background (discipline, reliability)
- Fairness focus (transparency, honesty)
- Partnership approach (not vendor relationship)
- Plain English explanations
- Proactive security mindset

---

## Presentation Structure

### Discovery Phase (40% of time)
**Listen more than talk**

Questions to ask:
- What's happening now that prompted this meeting?
- What have you tried already?
- What's the impact on your business?
- What does success look like?
- What concerns do you have about making a change?

### Context Sharing (30% of time)
**Your story, briefly**

- Quick background (military to IT)
- Why fairness matters
- How you work differently
- Examples relevant to their situation

### Solution Overview (20% of time)
**Tailored to what you learned**

- Address their specific pain points
- Explain your approach
- Set clear expectations
- Outline next steps
- Transparent about costs/timeline

### Close (10% of time)
**Clear call to action**

- Summarize what you heard
- Confirm next steps
- Answer questions
- Get commitment

---

## Adapting Military Stories for Prospects

### When to Use
- Building credibility
- Explaining your values
- Showing problem-solving approach
- Demonstrating reliability under pressure

### When to Skip
- They're in a hurry
- Highly technical discussion
- They've already decided (just need details)
- Follow-up meetings (they know your background)

### How to Frame It
Keep it relevant:
- "In the military, I learned [principle]. Here's how that applies to your situation..."
- "My background taught me to [approach]. For your business, that means..."

## Common Prospect Objections

### "You're more expensive than the other quotes"
**Response**: "Let me show you what's included in ours vs theirs. Often what looks cheaper upfront costs more in hidden fees, downtime, and problems down the road. I believe in transparent pricing - no surprises."

### "We need to think about it"
**Response**: "Absolutely. What specific questions can I answer while you're considering? What's your timeline for making a decision?"

### "Our current IT person is okay, just has some issues"
**Response**: "What would need to change for you to feel really confident in your IT support, not just 'okay' with it?"

### "We can't afford downtime during transition"
**Response**: "That's exactly why we plan transitions carefully. Here's how we ensure zero business disruption... [specific process]"

## Red Flags to Watch For

### In Them
- Unwilling to discuss budget
- Won't share current situation details
- Just price shopping
- Unrealistic expectations
- Disrespectful of current provider

### In You
- Talking more than listening
- Making promises you can't keep
- Overselling capabilities
- Dismissing their concerns
- Rushing to close

---

## Follow-Up Strategy

### Immediately After
- Send recap email
- Include any materials promised
- Clear next steps
- Thank them for time

### If They Go Quiet
- Follow up at agreed timeline
- Provide value (article, tip, resource)
- Don't be pushy
- Know when to move on

### If They Say Yes
- Set expectations for onboarding
- Introduce team if applicable
- Confirm timeline and deliverables
- Start building relationship

## Customization by Prospect Type

### Small Business (1-20 employees)
- Focus on: Affordability, reliability, simplicity
- Concerns: Cost, downtime, complexity
- Language: Plain English, practical examples
- Approach: Straightforward, friendly, accessible

### Medium Business (20-100 employees)
- Focus on: Scalability, security, efficiency
- Concerns: Growth, compliance, ROI
- Language: Business outcomes, metrics
- Approach: Professional, strategic, consultative

### Enterprise (100+ employees)
- Focus on: Integration, compliance, risk management
- Concerns: Security, uptime, vendor management
- Language: Technical when needed, strategic focus
- Approach: Formal, detailed, process-oriented
